## Key Decisions
[SOURCE: https://www.dmre.gov.za/Portals/0/Resource%20Center/Mine%20Health%20and%20Safety%20Act%20and%20its%20Regulations/MHS%20Regulations_Chapter%203_GG%2038708.pdf?ver=2018-03-13-015344-003] SA mine electricity regulations strongly default electrical work that requires approach/handling to “dead work”, only permitting live work under direct competent supervision, and require electrical apparatus to be under competent-person control. [SOURCE: https://www.angloamerican.com/~/media/Files/A/Anglo-American-Group-v9/PLC/sustainability/our-strategy/fatal-risk-standards.pdf] Major-operator fatal-risk standards operationalise this as a formal LOTO/PTW workflow (personal locks, tagging, formal handover, training/competence and audits), which is inherently higher-friction than a walk-up install. [SOURCE: http://www.acts.co.za/occupational-health-and-safe/nr250_5_notice] Industrial electrical machinery rules reinforce restricted access/authorisation around switchgear/distribution enclosures. [SOURCE: https://manuals.plus/ae/1005008915582551] Clamp-on CTs typically require encircling a single conductor, while motor/VFD cabling is often multi-conductor in one jacket, pushing feasible clamp points into controlled electrical spaces (MCCs, terminations, segregated single-core runs). [DERIVED: combine SA mine electrical regs + operator standards → install workflow constraint] Net: CT-based self-powered stickers are not viable for a low-friction “walk-and-stick” deployment model in SA mineral-processing plants; they should be treated as an electrician/PTW/LOTO-installed tier (often planned shutdown) or replaced with sensing modalities that do not require electrical access.
