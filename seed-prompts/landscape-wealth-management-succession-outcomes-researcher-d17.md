## Key Decisions
[DERIVED: synthesis of F912-F918] Q022 is answerable on public evidence with an explicit boundary. Public wealth-management sources do report retention-related outcomes from advisor succession and transitions, but the observable measures are mostly indirect or operational: asset-loss benchmarks by move type [SOURCE: https://www.cerulli.com/press-releases/new-wealth-management-research-finds-transition-support-services-critical-to-retaining-assets-during-advisor-moves], investor-switch risk when an advisor leaves [SOURCE: https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/financial-services/our-insights/the-looming-advisor-shortage-in-us-wealth-management], anecdotal high-retention advisor moves [SOURCE: https://www.financial-planning.com/news/how-advisors-use-technology-to-retain-clients-during-transitions], and RIA deal retention hurdles plus reported consent attainment [SOURCE: https://www.wealthmanagement.com/mergers-acquisitions/understanding-the-client-retention-hurdle-in-ria-sales]. Public custodian and succession-program pages are useful for showing planning prevalence and continuity emphasis, but in this scan they did not expose standardized post-close client- or asset-retention scorecards [SOURCE: https://advisorservices.schwab.com/insights-hub/perspectives/ria-succession-planning]. The practical answer is therefore that public evidence exists, but it is concentrated in asset/AUM retention, client-switch propensity, and transaction-consent thresholds rather than named program efficacy tables.
